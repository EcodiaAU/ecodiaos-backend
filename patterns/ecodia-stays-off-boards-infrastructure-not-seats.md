# Ecodia stays off boards - we own the infrastructure they sit on, not the seats they sit in

## Rule

When a client, partner, or aligned organisation offers Ecodia (or Tate as its representative) a board seat, advisory role, director position, or governance role: decline. Build the software, data, and operational infrastructure they need to govern well - do not sit at the table yourself.

## Why

Sitting on a board produces one thing: governance influence. Building infrastructure produces compounding leverage.

- A board seat is a time-cost with one org. Infrastructure deployed to that org is a revenue rail + credibility anchor + reference case, indefinitely.
- Board membership creates conflicts of interest. Ecodia building software for an org where we hold a board seat tangles procurement, skews impartiality, and complicates the commercial relationship. The software agreement becomes politicised.
- Governance influence is not Ecodia's edge. Our edge is speed, quality, and AI-native product development. Board rooms reward presence and consensus. That is not the game we play.
- The positioning "we build the infrastructure you govern with" is more powerful than "we sit at the table with you." It casts Ecodia as substrate - the operating layer every board outcome depends on - not as a peer voice in one room.
- This is the same reason Ecodia Labs retains IP and licenses it rather than assigning it: we hold the structural asset, the client relationship is layered on top.

## How

1. Acknowledge the offer warmly. It is a genuine signal of trust and regard.
2. Decline clearly. One sentence: "I want to keep Ecodia in the infrastructure seat, not the governance seat - it's a better fit for what we're building."
3. Counter with the infrastructure offer: what software, data, or operational tooling could Ecodia build that makes their governance function better?
4. Do not hedge. Accepting "just an advisory role" is the thin edge. Advisory roles become governance expectations become board-seat pressure. Bright line from the start.

## Exceptions

None currently. This rule does not have a "if the org is big enough" carve-out. Scale of the org is an argument for the infrastructure play being more valuable, not for taking the seat.
