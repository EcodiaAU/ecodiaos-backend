Merchandise with nothing on it.

EcodiaOS · 2026-06-01

A v1 plan for the secondhand-garments project. The name is Clothes. It lives at clothes.ecodia.au as its own small app, linked from ecodia.au but kept off the marketing site so the marketing site stays light. The garments are completely unbranded. They carry no logo or print of any kind, the stitching is whatever the original maker did, and the tags come off. The packaging carries only the tagline. The whole thing is a quiet joke that funds itself, and the joke is that the merch is just clothes.

the shape

Tate has a room of secondhand garments. None of them match. They become the inventory. Each piece is photographed, listed as its own one-of-a-kind product, and grouped with similar pieces into a drop that goes live as a single moment. When a drop sells out it closes; what remains rolls into the next drop's curation pool.

The customer arrives at clothes.ecodia.au, sees the current drop, picks a piece, checks out as a guest. The piece arrives in kraft paper with one line on the label. The world we build next. Nothing else. The shirt has nothing on it.

The joke does not need explaining. The customer either gets it or does not. The people who get it become the customers. The people who do not are not the audience.

the pipeline

The pipeline is built to be as automated as the process allows. Tate's only manual loop is the physical one: pick up a piece, photograph it from three or four angles in natural light against a neutral wall, and add the things only a human can know. Size, condition notes, vibe tag, anything unusual.

From there a scheduled process handles the rest. Each new piece gets a generated title, a generated description in Ecodia voice, a slug, an assignment to either the current drop or the next-in-queue drop, a Stripe product registration with quantity one, and a draft listing in the CMS waiting for a final go-live toggle. Drops go live on a schedule that Tate sets.

The substrate is a dedicated Supabase project called clothes and its storage bucket for the photos. The storefront is a small Next.js app in its own repo, on its own Vercel project, on its own subdomain. None of it touches the marketing site at ecodia.au, which stays the three-line static page it is. The two surfaces meet only through a link in the ecodia.au footer.

the storefront

Three route shapes. The drop landing page at clothes.ecodia.au shows the current drop as a grid of pieces with a one-sentence curation note. The piece detail page at clothes.ecodia.au/<slug> shows the photos, the size and condition, and a buy button that opens Stripe Checkout. The drop archive at clothes.ecodia.au/drop-<n> preserves past drops as a record of what existed once.

There are no accounts on the storefront. The cart and wishlist patterns common to ecommerce are not part of the design. Guest checkout via Stripe uses email as the identifier. A piece sells, the inventory updates, the listing flips to sold, and the simplicity is the point.

the packaging

Kraft paper outer, tissue inner. One label on the outside reading only "The World We Build Next" in Spectral italic. There is no card inside the parcel, and no insert explaining the joke. The garment itself arrives clean and pressed with no swing tags or brand stamps added. It is the way it was found.

The packaging is the brand because the garment is not. This is the same grammar as ecodia.au itself, where three lines of italic on white carry everything the brand needs to carry, and adding anything would make it worse.

the name

The product is called Clothes. That is the storefront name, the subdomain, and the line item on the invoice. It is one word, sentence-case where it appears in copy, and accurate to the contents. The earlier working title was Worn; it served until something more honest arrived. Clothes is more honest. The category and the product collapse into the same word, which is the joke without making the joke.

the marketing

The marketing is user-generated content, aggressively. EcodiaOS posts each drop on social as its own author in the same voice that runs the rest of the platform. Tate wears the pieces in his actual life. Customers wear them, photograph themselves wearing them, post the photos, and those photos become the content for the next drop's promotion. There is no paid acquisition channel.

An opt-in email list at the storefront tells people when the next drop lands. Nothing else. The newsletter, abandoned-cart, upsell, and winback patterns standard to ecommerce are explicitly absent. One email per drop, when the drop goes live.

what v1 does not decide

Economics are deferred. Margin split, where the profit routes, returns policy, international shipping. These are the questions the first drop will answer better than a planning document can. The first drop's job is to find out whether the joke works and whether the pieces sell. The answers shape the rest.

Cross-promotion with Nearby is deferred. The story of "this drop funds these merchants by name" is a v2 narrative, and a launch one would only dilute both. Build them both, see them both work, then connect them.

first moves

The plan-to-execution handoff lives in the status_board row id f191257b. The immediate next moves are: scaffold the clothes.ecodia.au Next.js app in its own repo, point a new Supabase project named clothes at it, wire the Stripe Checkout integration, build the photograph-to-listing automation as an Edge Function or a small scheduled cron, add a single footer link on ecodia.au pointing here, and decide which dozen pieces from the existing room form Drop 01.

The first drop sets the tone for every drop that follows. The work is to make the first one feel right.
