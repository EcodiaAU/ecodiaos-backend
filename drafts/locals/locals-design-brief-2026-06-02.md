# Locals.. design brief for a full visual rethink

## What it is

Locals. is a national network of local businesses. Customers find local places near them and redeem rewards by showing a 6-character code at the counter. Merchants pay what they want each month, from zero up; the platform never takes a cut. The whole point is to make it easier to choose the local option when you have the choice. Web app at locals.ecodia.au, native iOS + Android land after.

## What it should feel like

Reading a hand-set essay about your neighbourhood, not opening a SaaS dashboard. One purpose per screen. Italic prose where there would normally be a label and a field. The map is the brand experience for discovery; everything else is text.

The product belongs to the same family as Chambers (chambers.business) and Glovebox (glovebox.ecodia.au) and Clothes (clothes.ecodia.au). Same Spectral italic voice, same one-italic-sentence-as-the-screen-cue grammar, same refusal of pills and cards and dashboards. Look at those three before drawing.

## Palette

Cream on a dark saturated ground, same saturation register as Chambers (teal #2F5F69) and Glovebox (burnt-orange #A8431F). The ground was previously a pale honey #FCD58B which felt too light and washed the brand out. Pull it back to something with real saturation. The previous starting point was #C49A3F which was too gold; the target is mustard-ish but darker and richer. Pick a hex; we will iterate. Cream stays as #E8DFC9 (the family cream that Chambers and Glovebox both use). Ink #1F1810 is available for inverse-state surfaces.

The cream-on-ground combo should pass WCAG AA (4.5:1) for body text, which means the ground needs to be dark enough. Chambers teal hits 5.34:1 and Glovebox burnt-orange hits 4.54:1 with the same cream; aim for that range.

## Type

Spectral italic for display + body. The whole site reads italic. Inter as the fallback for system rendering only.

## What to drop

The dice motif (five dots in quincunx) is gone. It was branding-shaped and Ecodia does not do branding. The mark for the app icon needs a new idea entirely. Open territory.

Pills, cards, dashboards, sidebar nav, tabs, save buttons, label-field-hint form stacks. Replace with italic prose, inline links, sections that flow down a single column.

## What to keep

The map. Discover lives or dies by the map.
The redemption code. Six big characters, monospace, centered, with a 5-minute countdown. This is a high-stakes moment.
The owner note. A single italic paragraph the merchant edits, surfaces above their listing dated "from Sarah, 2h ago".
Pay-what-you-want billing. Inline italic input, no Stripe-styled form.
The five-minute-walk lens and first-visit reward as filter cues on Discover.

## App icon

Liquid Glass treatment is what iOS 26 applies automatically; the source needs to be a flat mark that holds up under the polish. Cream-on-the-new-ground, same family logic as the Chambers icon and Glovebox icon. The mark itself is the designer's call. Anything that says "this is a network of local places" without saying it literally.

## The shape of the redesign

Every screen could read like one of those three sibling sites' marketing landings: one italic sentence + a small entry-row + a short lede + footer. The functional pages (map, code, pulse editor, billing) are the exceptions that earn extra UI; everything else should be a column of italic prose with inline links.

If a designer is unsure whether something needs a button or an inline italic link, the answer is the inline italic link.
